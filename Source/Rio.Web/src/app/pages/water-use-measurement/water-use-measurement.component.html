1. Measuring Water Use

Measuring Water Use

Tracking water use and demand

Water use calculations on this trading platform are provided by OpenET - a transparent and easily-accessible web-based platform for credible estimates of consumptive water use at the field scale. OpenET relies on publicly available satellite and weather station data to estimate evapotranspiration (ET) - water that is transferred from the land to the atmosphere via evaporation from bare soil and transpiration through plant leaves. For irrigated agricultural fields, ET is necessary for plant growth and food production.

While several methods already exist for measuring ET at the field scale, they’re expensive, and since they’re calculated differently by every consulting group, there isn’t a lot of broad trust in the data. Rosedale-Rio Bravo chose to partner with OpenET because it is bringing some of the leading scientists and most commonly used approaches for estimating ET together in one place, helping to create a shared basis for decision-making.

OpenET also significantly increases access to ET data for individual farmers and landowners, giving them the opportunity to see the same data that water managers and regulators are using by providing low-cost, near real-time ET estimates through a widely accessible website. At this stage of development, the OpenET platform remains in a beta version, and data are being provided here for user testing purposes only. However, with further work on model intercomparison and refinements, the objective of OpenET is to provide data that can be both broadly accessed and broadly trusted, with ET estimates that are well-documented, easily retrieved, and truly representative of the best available science.
Rosedale Rio Bravo utilizes data from OpenET within this trading platform to account for consumptive use on each assessor’s parcel. This information allows the district to accurately account for and report the water consumptively used by each parcel, and gives landowners the ability to accurately track their water budgets throughout the year.

The OpenET Team

The OpenET project is being led by NASA, Desert Research Institute (DRI) and Environmental Defense Fund (EDF) with in-kind support from Google Earth Engine. The technical team includes approximately 30 researchers and practitioners from NASA, DRI, the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS), University of Nebraska, University of Idaho, University of Wisconsin, University of Maryland, California State University Monterey Bay, Google Earth Engine and the web development firm HabitatSeven, with expertise in satellite-based estimation of evapotranspiration, cloud computing, and user-driven website design.

Why will the data be revised?

Evapotranspiration (ET) information generated by OpenET are derived from various models that integrate satellite and meteorological data to generate estimates of ET at the field scale. Because they are estimates, and because the models utilize different methodologies to arrive at these estimates, the underlying OpenET models can produce results that diverge from field measurement. However, recent advances in remote sensing of ET have led to significant improvements in our ability to automate these methodologies, and many of these methods are now advancing towards operational use by U.S. state and federal agencies. The OpenET project is integrating many of these advances to support further improvements in water management by increasing access to remotely sensed ET data, and building confidence in the data by developing an ensemble value that is endorsed by a community of scientists. The models selected for inclusion in the initial ensemble are already informing water use decisions across the West, but the OpenET project makes it much easier to compare the models at scale, allowing identification of consistent differences between the various methods and approaches, and making it easier for the scientific community to further refine and improve the estimates. The OpenET team is currently initiating one of the largest model inter-comparison and accuracy assessments conducted to date to increase understanding of the uncertainty associated with ET data from individual models as well as to improve the ensemble-based estimates. The results from these intercomparison and accuracy assessment studies will inform selection of the final approach for calculation of a single value from the full ensemble of ET models.

The currently available data therefore represents the team’s initial estimate of an ensemble value based on the best available science. However, the science will significantly advance over the coming year as the inter-comparison effort progresses and additional lessons are learned.
