1. Measuring Water Use with OpenET

Measuring Water Use with OpenET

Tracking water use and demand

Water use calculations on this accounting platform are provided by OpenET - a web-based platform being developed to provide easily accessible, credible estimates of consumptive water use at the field scale. OpenET relies on publicly available satellite and weather data sets to estimate evapotranspiration (ET) - water that is transferred from the land to the atmosphere via evaporation from bare soil and transpiration through plant leaves. For irrigated agricultural fields, ET is necessary for plant growth and food production and is the largest consumptive use of water by the agricultural sector. The Landsat satellite is the primary satellite system used to produce ET information at the 30 m scale (pixel size).

While several methods already exist for measuring ET at the field scale, and since they can be calculated differently by every consulting group, there isn’t a lot of broad trust in the data. Rosedale-Rio Bravo chose to partner with OpenET because it is bringing leading scientists and some of the most commonly used approaches for estimating ET together in one place, in an open and transparent way, helping to create a shared basis for decision-making.

When it launches publicly in 2021, OpenET will significantly increase access to ET data for individual farmers and landowners, giving them the opportunity to see the same data that water managers and regulators are using by providing low-cost, near real-time ET estimates through a widely accessible website. The OpenET platform is currently in a beta version, and data are being provided here for user testing purposes only. However, with further work on model intercomparison and refinements, the objective of OpenET is to provide broadly trusted ET estimates that are well-documented, easily retrieved, and truly representative of the best available science.

Rosedale-Rio Bravo utilizes data from OpenET within this platform to account for consumptive water use within each assessor’s parcel. This information allows the district to accurately account for and report the water consumptively used by each parcel, and gives landowners the ability to accurately track their water budgets throughout the year.

The OpenET Team

The OpenET project is being led by NASA, Desert Research Institute (DRI) and Environmental Defense Fund with in-kind support from Google Earth Engine. The technical team includes approximately 30 researchers and practitioners from NASA, DRI, the U.S. Department of Agriculture, the U.S. Geological Survey, University of Nebraska, University of Idaho, University of Wisconsin, University of Maryland, California State University Monterey Bay, Google Earth Engine and the web development firm HabitatSeven, with expertise in satellite-based estimation of evapotranspiration, cloud computing, and user-driven website design.

Accuracy and Evolution of the Dataset

OpenET is providing evapotranspiration data derived from several models that integrate satellite and meteorological data to generate ET estimates at the field scale. Recent advances in remote sensing of ET have led to significant improvements in the ability to automate the operation of these methodologies, and many of these methods are already informing water use decisions across the western United States. The OpenET project is advancing understanding and trust in ET data by making it much easier to compare the models at scale. This allows for identification of differences between the various methods, and further refinement of the estimates.

As a critical component of this process, the OpenET team is initiating one of the largest model intercomparison and accuracy assessments conducted to date, and this effort will likely extend through the end of 2020. The results will inform the calculation of an improved ensemble value from the full suite of ET models, developed and endorsed by the community of scientists working on OpenET.

The data currently in the accounting platform represent the team’s preliminary estimate of an ensemble value. However, please keep in mind that the science and accuracy will likely advance over the coming year as the intercomparison effort progresses, and as additional lessons are learned and approaches are improved.
